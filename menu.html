Types of Sustainable farming

Organic Farming

Organic farming is a farming practice that doesn't use synthetic inputs like pesticides and fertilizers. Instead, it uses traditional inputs like: Green manure, Compost manure, Crop rotation, Biological pest control, Intercropping.
Organic farming also:
 1. Uses biological fertilizers and pest control from animal or plant waste
 2. Strives for sustainability
 3. Enhances soil fertility and biological diversity
 4. Prohibits synthetic pesticides, antibiotics, synthetic fertilizers, genetically modified organisms, and growth hormones
Organic farming was started as a response to the environmental problems caused by chemical pesticides and synthetic fertilizers. The British botanist Sir Albert Howard is often called the father of modern organic agriculture.
Organic farming has some benefits, like:
 1. Ecological benefits
 2. Higher food costs
 3. Generally lower yields

Agroforestry

Agroforestry is a land management system that combines agriculture and forestry. It involves growing trees or shrubs around or among crops or pastureland.
Benefits of agroforestry
 1. Healthier soil
 2. Higher yields
 3. Shade for crops
 4. Prevents soil erosion
 5. Creates homes for wildlife
 6. Contributes to a green economy
 7. Promotes long-term, sustainable, and renewable forest management
Other benefits of agroforestry
 1. It's a practical and low-cost means of implementing many forms of integrated land management.
 2. It can help build resilience to climate change.
 3. It can help governments, non-governmental organizations, and farmers expand the scale of agroforestry for food security and climate.
The oldest known agroforestry practice
 1. Swidden or shifting cultivation
 2. It dates back to the beginnings of the human transition from the hunting and gathering phase to the plant domestication and cultivation system of livelihood.

Crop Rotation

Crop rotation is the practice of growing different crops in the same area over a series of growing seasons. The goal of crop rotation is to:
 1. Manage soil fertility
 2. Avoid or reduce problems with soil-borne diseases and some soil-dwelling insects
 3. Improve soil health
 4. Optimize nutrients in the soil
 5. Combat pest and weed pressure
Crop rotation reduces the reliance of crops on one set of nutrients, pest and weed pressure, and the probability of developing resistant pests and weeds. It's the opposite of continuous cropping, which is growing the same crop in the same field year after year.
Ideally, crops in a rotation are not of the same family. For example, legumes are useful in crop rotation because they have nodulated roots that harbor nitrogen fixing Rhizobium bacteria. When the roots are left over in fields and only aerial parts of legume crops are harvested, it leaves more nitrates in the soil.

Premaculture

Permaculture is a sustainable agricultural system that mimics natural ecosystems. It aims to reduce waste, prevent pollution, and improve biodiversity.
The term "permaculture" was coined in 1978 by Bill Mollison and David Holmgren. They formulated the concept in opposition to modern industrialized methods.
How to start a permaculture garden :
 1. Acquaint yourself with your surroundings
 2. Choose plants based on your environment
 3. Design your garden layout
 4. Build your garden beds
 5. Plant your permaculture garden
 6. Add a layer of organic mulch to the topsoil
 7. Add compost without disturbing the soil

Aquaculture

Aquaculture is the process of farming aquatic organisms, such as fish, shellfish, and aquatic plants. It's similar to agriculture, but with fish instead of plants or livestock.
What is aquaculture?
 1. Breeding, growing, and harvesting: Aquaculture involves breeding, raising, and harvesting aquatic organisms in water.
 2. Environmentally responsible: Aquaculture can help to create healthier habitats and rebuild populations of endangered species.
 3. Human consumption: Aquaculture businesses prepare plants and animals for human consumption.
Risks of aquaculture :
 1.  Disease transmission: Disease can be transmitted between farmed and wild fish.
 2. Pathogens and parasites: Nonnative pathogens and parasites can be introduced when fish are transported.
Asia is the largest aquaculture producer In 2017, Asia accounted for 92% of the live-weight volume of animals and seaweeds.

Hydroponics

Hydroponics is a method of growing plants without soil. Instead, plants are grown in a nutrient-rich solution that contains a mixture of basic, secondary, and micro-nutrients. The roots of the plants are supported by substances such as peat moss, clay pellets, perlite, and rockwool.
Benefits of hydroponics
 1. Uses less water than traditional soil-based systems
 2. Allows for faster growth and higher yields than traditional soil-based growing systems
 3. Can grow hydroponically all year long
Challenges of hydroponics :
 1. There is a steep learning curve
 2. Small errors can affect the whole crop
 3. The systems are very vulnerable to equipment failure or power outage, which can kill the plants within a few hours
 4. Yields are about the same as for soil-grown crops

Urban Agriculture

Urban agriculture is the practice of growing food in cities. It can include:
Growing crops, Raising livestock, Processing and distributing food, Animal husbandry, Aquaculture, Beekeeping, Horticulture.
Urban agriculture can take place in small areas like: Vacant plots, Gardens, Verges, Balconies, Containers.
Urban agriculture can provide food and income for urban dwellers. It can also:
 1. Promote local and sustainable food systems
 2. Improve the environment, ecosystem, and climate
 3. Bring communities together
 4. Improve the health of citizens
 5. Create intergenerational bonds
 6. Provide education, skill-building, and job training

Polyculture

Polyculture is a farming practice that involves growing multiple crops or animal species in the same space at the same time. It's also known as intercropping.
Benefits of polyculture :
Reduced pest damage
 1. Soil conservation
 2. Increased crop yields per area
 3. Increased diversity
 4. Improved productivity
 5. Self-sustainable pest-management regime
 6. Promotes biodiversity
 7. Enhances ecological resilience
 8. Reduces the risk of crop failure
Examples of polyculture

 3. Tilapia, wuchang fish, crucian carp, red eye, white amur bream, snake head, and mariadarin fish
Polyculture vs. monoculture
 1. Polyculture: Grows multiple crops or animal species in the same space at the same time.
 2. Grows only one plant or animal species together.
Other benefits of polyculture
 1. More environmentally friendly
 2.  More profitable
 3. More resilient

Natural Farming

Natural farming is a chemical-free farming system that uses the laws of nature to encourage biodiversity. It integrates crops, trees, and livestock.
Features of natural farming
 1. Chemical-free: No chemical or organic fertilizers are added to the soil.
 2. Biodiversity: Natural farming works with the natural biodiversity of each farmed area. It encourages the complexity of living organisms, both plants and animals, to thrive along with food plants.
 3. Soil quality: Natural farming uses natural bio-inoculums instead of chemical fertilizers and pesticides. It exploits beneficial microorganisms to enhance soil quality and soil health.
History of natural farming
 1. Natural farming is rooted in Indian tradition.
 2. It's enriched with modern understanding of ecology, resource recycling, and on-farm resource optimization.
 3. Yoshikazu Kawaguchi is widely regarded as the leading practitioner of the second-generation of natural farmers. He instigated the Akame Natural Farm School, and a related network of volunteer-based "no-tuition" natural farming schools in Japan.

Contour Ploughing

Contour ploughing is a farming technique that involves following the natural contours of the land when tilling, planting, and cultivating. It helps to prevent soil erosion by slowing down the flow of water and preventing it from washing away topsoil.
Benefits of contour ploughing
 1. Reduces soil erosion
 2. Controls runoff water
 3. Increases moisture infiltration and retention
 4. Improves water distribution in crops
 5. Prevents tillage erosion
 6. Enhances soil quality and composition
Drawbacks of contour ploughing
 1. Pesticides used to manage weeds can kill surrounding grass, increasing the possibility of water runoff.
 2. Contour farming loses its effectiveness with increases in the slope gradient, slope length, and rainfall intensity.
 3. History of contour ploughing Contour ploughing was first used by the Phoenicians in what is now Lebanon sometime during the period of 1200 to 900 BC. The practice slowly spread to many surrounding regions and eventually reached Europe.
